## Cutting Edge Chapter 16: Self-Driving Cars

### 【Paragraph 1】
The number of road deaths in Japan in 2018 was a record low of 3,532. The National Police Agency reported that this reduction since the peak in 1970 of over 16,000 was **(1) due to** more traffic safety education. The **introduction** of seat belts, air bags and other safety equipment **(2) into** cars since 1970 is also likely to be a factor.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(1) due to**: 「～が原因で」。*caused by* に近い。
> *   **(2) introduction A into B**: AをBに「導入」する。前置詞 *into* が狙われる。

### 【Paragraph 2】
Three thousand five hundred is still a high number, **(A) averaging** roughly ten per day. Many people dream that self-driving cars (SDC) will greatly increase road safety. **To date**, the accidents **(B) involving** SDCs have been caused by humans. The hope is that when all cars are SDCs, human error will be **eradicated**, and road deaths become a thing of the past.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(A) averaging**: 分詞構文。「平均すると～になる」。能動的な意味なので *ing*。
> *   **To date**: 「現在までのところ」。
> *   **(B) involving**: 直前の *accidents* を修飾。「SDCを巻き込む（関与させる）事故」。*involved*（巻き込まれた）と間違えやすいが、*involve* は「～を含む、伴う」という他動詞なので、事故がSDCを含んでいる＝能動 *involving*。
> *   **eradicated**: = **removed**（取り除く、撲滅する）。

### 【Paragraph 3】
**(3) [ However pleasant this idea may be, it remains a dream. ]**
SDCs need to be **programmed** to predict as many situations as possible to reduce danger. Certainly, unexpected situations will still occur, such as people breaking the law **(4) at** red lights and walking into the oncoming traffic. A major question arises; when danger cannot be **circumvented**, how should SDCs **(C) be programmed** to respond?

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(3) 整序・和訳**: 「この考えがどんなに魅力的であっても、それはまだ夢のままだ。」
>     *   *However + 形容詞 + S + V* = 譲歩「どんなに～でも」。
>     *   *remain* = 「～のままである」。
> *   **(4) at red lights**: 「赤信号で」。地点・状況の *at*。
> *   **circumvented**: = **avoided**（回避する、避ける）。
> *   **(C) be programmed**: 主語は *SDCs*。プログラム「される」側なので受動態。

### 【Paragraph 4】
The English philosopher Phillipa Foot introduced a thought experiment called *the Trolley Problem*. A driverless train is going along a track which **divides (5) into** two branches. On one branch, five people **are tied up** on the track. On the other branch, one person **is tied up**. The train cannot be stopped, and it will hit the five people. A switch controls **which branch the train travels**.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(5) divide into**: 「～に分かれる」。変化・結果の *into*。
> *   **are tied up / is tied up**: 「縛り付けられている」。受動態。主語の単数・複数（*people* vs *person*）に注意。
> *   **which branch the train travels**: ここでの *travels* は**他動詞**。「（特定の経路）を進む」。直後に目的語 *which branch* を取っている構造を見抜くこと。

### 【Paragraph 5】
You have two options: 1) to press the switch to move the train **onto** the other track. This means that one person will be hit, but that you will have **initiated** this; 2) to do nothing. In this case, five people will be hit, but you will not be involved in the accident. In tests, most people say that they will press the switch and save five lives.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **onto**: *move ... onto ~*（～の上へ移動させる）。
> *   **initiated**: 「着手した、引き起こした」。

### 【Paragraph 6】
The *Trolley Problem* is useful for future SDC technology because it **(6) [ gets people to think about issues that are important in road safety ].** Should SDCs be programmed to, for example, save more or fewer lives? The options can be changed to other choices. Should young people’s lives **(D) be saved** **(7) instead of** old people’s? Females or males? Doctors or homeless people? Healthy or unhealthy? The list goes on. A team of scientists from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) created a website to ask these questions.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(6) 整序**: *get + O + to do* = 「Oに～させる」。関係代名詞 *that* が *issues* を修飾。
> *   **(D) be saved**: 主語は *lives*。救われる側なので受動態。
> *   **(7) instead of**: 「～の代わりに」。

### 【Paragraph 7】
By 2018, the MIT team had collected over 40 million responses from all over the world. The results were **intriguing**: females are more important than males; younger people more valuable than older people; high-status citizens more meaningful than low-status individuals. Differences between cultures were also observed. For example, Eastern countries, including Japan, preferred to save the lives of lawful people, but Southern countries, such as Brazil, did not.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **intriguing**: = **interesting**（興味深い）。

### 【Paragraph 8】
So far, however, these tests are only thought experiments. **(8) [ What people say they will do and what they actually do may be very different. ]** What we say is **based (9) on** how we think. But are we really the people we think we are? In 2018, an American TV show *Mind Field* tested this question with real people. Making this show was very dangerous in terms of morals because it can cause severe psychological damage to the people in the study.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(8) 整序・和訳**: 「人々がそうすると言うことと、実際に行うこととは、大きく違うかもしれない。」
>     *   主語が *[What people say (that) they will do] and [what they actually do]* という2つの名詞節の塊。
> *   **(9) be based on**: 「～に基づいている」。

### 【Paragraph 9】
The information **(E) learned** from the show, however, indicated that many people would be paralyzed; they could do nothing. The message for SDCs is highly complex. From now on, there needs to be a serious discussion between governments, car manufacturers and consumer groups about how SDCs should respond to unusual situations. **This** is likely to be an intriguing and extremely valuable discussion.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(E) learned**: *The information (which was) learned...*。「得られた情報」。過去分詞の後置修飾。
> *   **This**: 指示語の内容は「SDCが異常事態にどう対応すべきかについての、政府・メーカー・消費者グループ間での真剣な議論」。

## Cutting Edge Chapter 17: Sleeping Patterns

### 【Paragraph 1】
We often worry about **(A) lying** awake in the middle of the night — but it may not be a problem. A **growing body of** evidence from both science and history suggests that a long and uninterrupted sleep may not be natural.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(A) lying**: *about*（前置詞）の目的語なので動名詞。*lie*（横になる）→ *lying*（スペル変化に注意）。
> *   **a growing body of**: 「一連の、大量の」。

### 【Paragraph 2】
In the early 1990s, researcher Thomas Wehr conducted an experiment in which a group of people were kept in darkness for 14 hours every day for a month. **(7) [ It took some time for their sleep to become regular. ]** But by the fourth week the subjects had **settled (1) into** a very distinct sleeping pattern. They slept first for four hours, then woke for one or two hours before **(B) falling** into a second four-hour sleep.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(7) 整序**: *It takes + 時間 + for A + to do* = 「Aが～するのに（時間）がかかる」。
> *   **(1) settle into**: 「～（の状態）に落ち着く、定着する」。
> *   **(B) falling**: *before*（前置詞）の目的語なので動名詞。

### 【Paragraph 3】
Though sleep scientists paid attention to the results of the study, **(8) [ the idea that we must sleep for eight continuous hours persists among the general public. ]** More recently, in 2001, historian Roger Ekirch of Virginia Tech published an influential paper, **(C) drawn (2) from** 16 years of research, revealing a lot of historical evidence that humans used to sleep in two distinct periods. His book *At Day’s Close: Night in Times Past*, published four years later, presents more than 500 **references (3) to** a split sleeping pattern — in diaries, court records, medical books, and literature, from ancient Greece to modern Africa.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(8) 整序**:
>     *   *The idea that ...* = 同格のthat。「～という考え」。
>     *   *persists* = 「根強く残る、持続する」。
> *   **(C) drawn**: *paper (which was) drawn from...* = 「～から導き出された論文」。過去分詞。
> *   **(2) drawn from**: 「～から（情報を）引く、～に基づく」。
> *   **(3) reference to**: 「～への言及」。

### 【Paragraph 4】
**Much like the experience of Wehr’s subjects**, these references describe a first sleep which began about two hours after sunset, **(D) followed (4) by** a waking period of one or two hours and then a second sleep. “(9) **[ It’s not just the number of references to this sleeping pattern that is significant ]**, but it’s also the way they refer to it, **as if it (E) were** common knowledge,” Ekirch says.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **Much like...**: Wehrの実験の被験者と同様に、「最初に4時間眠り、1〜2時間起きて、また4時間眠る」というパターンのこと。
> *   **(D) followed by**: 分詞構文。「（最初の睡眠に）続いて～があった」。
> *   **(9) 整序**: *It is ... that ...* の強調構文。*not just A (but also B)* = 「Aだけでなく（Bも）」。
> *   **(E) were**: *as if*（まるで～かのように）の中なので**仮定法過去**。事実に反する（今は常識ではない）ため。

### 【Paragraph 5】
**(5) During** this waking period, people did all kinds of things. They often got up, went to the toilet or smoked tobacco and some even visited neighbors. People read, wrote, and often prayed. Countless prayer manuals from the late 15th century offered special prayers for the hours in between sleeps. Ekirch found that references to the first and second sleep began to disappear during the late 17th century. This started among the urban upper classes in northern Europe, and over the course of the next 200 years gradually influenced the rest of Western society. By the 1920s, the idea of a first and second sleep had disappeared completely.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(5) During**: 「（特定の期間）の間」。

### 【Paragraph 6】
Ekirch gives the reasons for the initial shift as improvements in street lighting, home lighting and an increase in coffee houses, which were sometimes open all night. As the night became a time for increased activity, **the length of time people could spend resting decreased**. **(6) With** the introduction of street lighting, socializing at night slowly became common among all social classes.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **spend resting**: 文法構造に注意。*spend + O + (in) -ing*。
>     *   先行詞: *the length of time*
>     *   関係詞節: *(that) people could spend resting*（人々が休息に費やすことのできる～）
>     *   *resting* は動名詞。*spend* の目的語 *time* が前に出ている。
> *   **(6) With**: 「～の導入に伴って」。付帯状況。

### 【Paragraph 7】
In 1667, Paris became the first city in the world to light its streets, using wax candles in glass lamps. It was followed by Amsterdam two years later, where a much more efficient oil-powered lamp was developed. London did not install street lighting until 1684, but by the end of the century, more than 50 of Europe’s major towns and cities **(F) were lit** at night. Nighttime activities became fashionable, so people went to bed later, and only had one period of sleep.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(F) were lit**: *light*（照らす）の受動態。過去分詞は *lit* または *lighted*。

□ **It took time for A to do** (時間がかかる)

## Cutting Edge Chapter 18: The Paradox of Happiness

### 【Paragraph 1】
There is a paradox at the heart of our lives. Most people want more income and work hard for it. Yet, as Western societies have got richer, their people have become no happier. This is no old wives’ tale. It is a fact **(A) proven (1) by** many pieces of scientific research. As I’ll show, we have good ways to measure how happy people are, and all the evidence says that **(2) on** average people are no happier today than people were fifty years ago. **(7) [ At the same time, however, average incomes have doubled. ]** This paradox is equally true for the United States, Britain and Japan.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(A) proven**: 「証明された事実」。過去分詞の後置修飾。
> *   **(1) by**: 受動の意味上の主語（動作主）。
> *   **(2) on average**: 「平均して」。
> *   **(7) 整序**: 挿入句の *however* の位置と、完了形 *have doubled*（倍増した）に注意。

### 【Paragraph 2】
But aren’t our lives infinitely more comfortable? Indeed: we have more food, more clothes, more cars, more holidays and, above all, better health. Yet we are not happier. **(3) Despite** all the efforts of governments, teachers and doctors which have improved our lives, human happiness has not increased.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(3) Despite**: 「～にもかかわらず」。前置詞なので後ろには名詞句（*all the efforts...*）が続く。

### 【Paragraph 3】
Suppose you **(B) were asked** to **choose (4) between (C) living** in two imaginary worlds, in which prices were the same. **In the first of these worlds** you would get $50,000 a year, **(D) compared (5) with** an average income of $25,000. In the second of these worlds you would get $100,000 a year, compared with an average income of $250,000. Which do you choose? This question was put to a group of Harvard students and the majority preferred the first type of world. **(8) [ They were happy to be poorer, if their relative position improved. ]** People care greatly about their relative income, and they would be willing to accept a significant fall as long as they could improve their position in relation to other people.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(B) were asked**: *Suppose (that)...* の中。仮定法過去（ありえない設定）+ 受動態（尋ねられる）。
> *   **(4) choose between A and B**: 「AとBの間で選ぶ」。
> *   **(C) living**: *between*（前置詞）の目的語なので動名詞。
> *   **(D) compared with**: 「～と比較すると」。分詞構文。
> *   **In the first...**: 学生がこちらを選んだ理由は「周囲（平均）よりも相対的に高い収入が得られるから」。
> *   **(8) 整序**: *be happy to do*（喜んで～する、～で満足する）。*if* 節内も仮定法過去（*improved*）。

### 【Paragraph 4】
People also compare their income with what they themselves are used to. When they are asked how much income they need, richer people always say they need more than poorer people. So **(9) [ whether you are happy with your income depends on how it compares with some standard. ]** And that standard **depends (6) on** two things: what other people get and what you yourself are accustomed to getting. In the first case your feelings are governed by social comparison, and **in the second by your ability to get used to things**.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(9) 整序**:
>     *   主語：*Whether you are happy with your income*（収入に満足しているかどうか）
>     *   動詞：*depends on*（～による）
>     *   目的語：*how it compares with...*（それが基準とどう比較されるか＝どう匹敵するか）。*compare* はここでは自動詞「匹敵する」。
> *   **(6) depend on**: 「～次第である、～による」。
> *   **in the second...**: *In the second (case), your feelings are governed by...* の省略構造。

### 【Paragraph 5】
Because these two forces are so strong in human nature, it is quite difficult for economic growth to improve our happiness. The reason for this is that **as** actual incomes rise, **the standard by which income is judged** rises in step. You can see this from data collected by the Gallup Poll in the United States over many years. People were asked, “What is the smallest amount of money a family of four needs to get along reasonably well in this community?”

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **as**: 「～につれて（比例）」。
> *   **by which**: 先行詞は *the standard*。*judge income by the standard* の関係。

### 【Paragraph 6】
The chart shows how much money people thought they needed to live comfortably — that is, their “required income,” and how much money they actually earn — that is, their “actual income.” According to the chart, as people’s actual income increases, the amount of money they say they need also increases. So even though people become richer, they are never satisfied. They never say, “I earn much more than I need, so I am happy.” They simply feel that they need more money to be happy. This explains why people become no happier even when their standard of living improves.

### 【Paragraph 7】
These facts are truly depressing, because they mean that we cannot be happier however much money we might get. But at the same time, they teach us a useful lesson: financial success is not always necessary for your happiness, and to find another aspect of life that satisfies you is an easier way of becoming happy.

## Cutting Edge Chapter 3: Eye Size and Brain Power

### 【Paragraph 1】
**(1) A** study suggests that people who live at higher latitudes have larger eyes and more ability in their brains to **(A) deal (2) with** visual information **(B) compared (3) with** those **(C) living** nearer the equator. Researchers measured the brain volumes and eye sizes of 55 skulls kept at the Oxford University Museum of Natural History **(D) dating (4) from** the 19th century. The skulls represented 12 different populations **(5) from** around the world, including native people from England, Australia, China, Kenya, Micronesia and Scandinavia.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(A) deal with**: 「～を処理する、扱う」。不定詞の形容詞的用法 *ability to deal with...*。
> *   **(B) compared with**: 「～と比較して」。
> *   **(C) living**: *those living...* = *those (people who are) living...*。「住んでいる人々」。
> *   **(D) dating from**: 「～（の時代）にさかのぼる、～から始まる」。
> *   **(1) A study**: 不特定多数の中の1つの研究。

### 【Paragraph 2】
“As you move away from the equator, there’s less and less light available, so humans have had to evolve bigger and bigger eyes,” said Ellie Pearce, a graduate student from Oxford University and the lead author on the study. “Their brains also need to be bigger to deal with the extra visual input. **Having bigger brains doesn’t mean that higher-latitude humans are smarter**; it just means they need bigger brains to be able to see well where they live.”

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **和訳**: 「より大きな脳を持つということは、高緯度に住む人間の方がより賢いということを意味するのではない。」
>     *   動名詞主語 *Having...*。否定文 *doesn't mean that...* に注意。

### 【Paragraph 3】
This suggests that someone from Greenland and someone from Kenya will have the same ability to detect detail, but the person from the higher latitude needs more brainpower and bigger eyes to deal with the lower light levels.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **内容理解**:
>     *   **Greenland vs Kenya**: 細部を見分ける能力（視力）は**同じ**。
>     *   違いは「光の量」。高緯度（Greenland）は光が少ないため、同じように見るためにより大きな脳と目が必要。

### 【Paragraph 4】
Professor Robin Dunbar, also from Oxford University and a co-author of the study, said that people whose ancestors lived **(6) within** the Arctic Circle have eyeballs 20% bigger than people whose ancestors lived near the equator. They have an associated increase in the size of the visual part of the brain, **(7) [ which previous studies have shown matches with the size of the eyeball ].** Brain volume increases with latitude: people living at high latitudes north and south of the equator have bigger brains than people living near the equator.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(7) 整序**:
>     *   *which matches with...*（それが～と一致する）という関係詞節の中に、*previous studies have shown*（先行研究が示したところによると）が挿入されている構造。
>     *   先行詞: *an associated increase...* （または *the size of the visual part...*）。
>     *   *matches* の主語は *which*。

### 【Paragraph 5】
The results, **(E) published** in the journal *Biology Letters*, showed that the biggest brains, **(F) averaging** 1,484 milliliters, were from Scandinavia, **(8) while** the smallest brains, around 1,200 milliliters, came from Micronesia. The average eye size was 27 milliliters in Scandinavia and 22 milliliters in Micronesia. Professor Dunbar concluded that the increase in brain volume must have evolved relatively recently in human history.

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(E) published**: *results, (which were) published...* 「発表された結果」。
> *   **(F) averaging**: *brains, (which were) averaging...* 「平均して～になる脳」。能動。
> *   **(8) while**: 「一方で」。対比。

### 【Paragraph 6】
He added, “**(9) [ It’s only within the last 10,000 years or so ]** that modern humans have occupied all latitudes right up to the Arctic Circle. So, this is probably a development that’s happened **(10) within** the last 10,000 years.”

> **💡 Check Point**
> *   **(9) 整序**: *It is ... that ...* の強調構文。「ほんの過去1万年かそこらのことだ」。
> *   **(10) within**: 「～（の期間）以内に」。
